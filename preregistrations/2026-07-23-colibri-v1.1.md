# Pre-registration: predictions for colibri v1.1.0 (before public benchmarks exist)

**Author:** Federico Sciuca ([quantprobe](https://github.com/FedericoTs/quantprobe))
**Date staked:** 2026-07-23 (the git commit publishing this file is the timestamp)
**Target:** [colibri v1.1.0/v1.1.1](https://github.com/JustVugg/colibri/releases), released 2026-07-22
**Status:** OPEN — no community benchmarks of the new features existed when this was written

> **Disclaimer — experimental research.** Everything on my side is still experimental: the four laws were measured on one 2016 desktop plus published third-party numbers, the η bands carry stated error bars (±25–40% at extrapolation distance), and these predictions are exactly how young laws get stress-tested. Ratio predictions (P1, P2, P4) are the most portable; P3 and P5 lean on my own unpublished measurements and are flagged with their risk. Misses will be published with the same prominence as hits — a law you only confirm is a law you haven't tested.

## Why this document exists

My method is pre-registration: write the number down **before** the measurement, in public, where it can be checked. Prior record, all called in advance: Qwen3-30B hybrid **predicted 19 / measured 19.30 ± 0.88**; 110B-from-SATA **predicted 0.2–0.3 / measured 0.19**; XMP RAM scaling **predicted ×1.41+ / measured ×1.52**; Laguna S 2.1 on DGX Spark **predicted ~47 from the config alone / their published number 47.5 (within 1%)**; colibri v1.0's own published tiers **retrodicted inside the law's η bands**.

colibri v1.1 shipped yesterday with four features that land directly on the [tiered decode law](../LAWS.md):

> **tok/s = η(tier) × bandwidth ÷ active-bytes-per-token**, η(disk) measured 0.88–1.0

So here are the predictions, staked before anyone measures. If they hold, the law generalizes to code I've never run on hardware I don't own — again. If they miss, the misses are data and I'll publish them as such.

---

## P1 — Dual-SSD / N-drive expert sharding: near-linear, ×1.8–2.0 for two equal drives

**Claim.** On configurations where decode is disk-bandwidth-bound (the headline ~25 GB-RAM setup), streaming experts from two equal-bandwidth drives multiplies decode speed by the aggregate-bandwidth ratio: **×1.8–2.0** for two identical drives (≤10% striping/scheduling loss off the ideal ×2.0).

**Why.** In the disk-bound regime, time-per-token ≈ (uncached expert bytes) ÷ (disk bandwidth). The LRU/pinned cache serves its fraction from RAM at negligible cost either way, so doubling disk bandwidth halves the dominant term regardless of hit rate — *as long as the residual disk stream, not RAM or compute, is the bottleneck*.

**Refuted if:** two equal drives yield <×1.7 on a genuinely disk-bound config (reported with drive specs and cache-RAM size), or the scaling is not ~linear in aggregate bandwidth for N=2→3.
**Scope caveat:** configs with enough RAM that the hot working set is mostly cached are *not* disk-bound; sub-linear results there are expected and don't test this claim.

## P2 — int3-g64 (fmt=5): speed ×1.25–1.35 over int4 on disk-bound tiers; quality cost real but tolerable

**Claim.** Switching routed experts int4 → int3-g64 multiplies disk-bound decode by the byte ratio: ~4.25 effective bits → ~3.25 (assuming ~16-bit group scales at the stated group sizes), i.e. **×1.25–1.35**. Quality: a measurable perplexity cost — int3 sits between int4 and the ~2-bit data-free floor of Law 2 — but no collapse (collapse is a 1-bit phenomenon; we measured +253 ppl at 1-bit vs graceful degradation at 2-bit).

**Refuted if:** disk-bound speedup lands outside ×1.2–1.4 with format overheads accounted, or int3 collapses the model (would break Law 2's floor placement).

## P3 — E8/IQ3 lattice (fmt=6) buys almost nothing over scalar int3 at matched rate ⚠️ *the risky one*

**Claim.** At matched bits-per-weight on expert FFN tensors, the E8/lattice format improves quality only marginally over scalar grouped quantization: **≤0.05 effective bits of gain** (in perplexity-equivalent terms).

**Why.** I measured the element stream of MoE FFN weight tensors to be spectrally near-white — ~0 dB intra-tensor coding gain — which is precisely the regime where vector/lattice codecs cannot beat scalar quantizers by more than the packing bound. This is the least-hedged prediction here: it comes from my own unpublished measurement, and lattice formats are widely expected to win. If E8/IQ3 shows a *large* quality gain at matched rate on experts, my whiteness measurement doesn't generalize and I'll say so.

**Refuted if:** matched-rate comparisons show lattice beating scalar by the equivalent of >0.15 bits on expert tensors.

## P4 — The +13% AVX-512 decode gain will NOT reproduce on disk-bound configs

**Claim.** Their measured +13% AVX-512 decode improvement is a **RAM-tier effect** (compute/η-side). On disk-bandwidth-bound configs the same change moves end-to-end decode by **<5%**, because the bottleneck is the drive, not the ALUs.

**Why.** This is the sharpest everyday consequence of tiered decode: optimizations only pay on the tier that binds. It's also the cheapest prediction here for anyone to test — same build, same flags, two storage configs.

**Refuted if:** disk-bound configs show ≥8% decode gain from the AVX-512 path alone.

## P5 — MTP speculation on a bandwidth-bound MoE: modest at best, inverts under pressure

**Claim.** (a) Single-stream, disk-bound, MTP-1: expert bytes fetched **per accepted token** rise ×1.3–1.8 (speculated positions union near-disjoint expert sets — routing is flat, Jaccard 1.00 across domains in my measurements), so net speedup stays **≤×1.4** — well under what raw acceptance rates suggest. (b) Under multi-stream load or reduced cache RAM, the union pressure thrashes the expert cache and MTP **inverts to a net slowdown** — the speculation×MoE antagonism I measured at ×2.3-class on my own hardware, and the mechanism their own v1.1 changelog fights ("pilot speculation eviction guard restored effectiveness").

**Refuted if:** MTP alone yields >×1.6 end-to-end on a disk-bound config, or shows no degradation as streams/cache-pressure rise.

## Fair-test protocol (what I'd accept as a measurement)

Same model + weights + prompts across arms; report: drive model(s) and measured sequential bandwidth, RAM size and cache/pinning settings, stream count, and single-arm changes only (one feature toggled at a time). Ratios matter more than absolutes — η cancels in ratios, which is what makes these predictions portable to hardware I've never touched.

## What this is not

Not a criticism of colibri — the opposite. Their v1.1 features are *exactly* what the placement laws say a disk-streaming MoE engine should build next (more disk bandwidth, fewer active bytes, cache-aware speculation). Two independent projects converging on the same physics is the strongest evidence the physics is real. If JustVugg or anyone in their community runs these tests, I'll link the results here — hits *and* misses.
